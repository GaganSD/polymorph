# Semantic Graphing and Co-reference Resolution at Scale: Ultra-Fast Techniques for Dependency Preservation in Middleware Compression

## The Architectural Imperative for Semantic Preservation in Prompt Compression

The exponential scaling of Large Language Models (LLMs) has ushered in an era where context windows routinely exceed 128,000 tokens, enabling sophisticated applications in retrieval-augmented generation (RAG), episodic memory tracking, and multi-document synthesis. However, this expanded capacity introduces a severe infrastructural bottleneck: the Key-Value (KV) cache. During the generation phase, the memory required to store the KV states of past tokens scales linearly with sequence length and batch size, rapidly exhausting the high-bandwidth memory (HBM) of modern GPUs and precipitating unacceptable latency in enterprise inference gateways. To mitigate these computational and economic burdens, prompt compression and token dropping algorithms have been deployed as middleware interventions. These systems operate primarily during or immediately prior to the prefill stage, routinely excising 50% to 80% of the input tokens to maintain a fixed cache size. Historically, the dominant paradigm for this compression—exemplified by frameworks such as LLMLingua and SelectiveContext—has relied on lightweight, causal language models to calculate the self-information (perplexity) of individual lexical units. Tokens exhibiting low perplexity are deemed highly predictable given their local context, characterized as having low information entropy, and are subsequently discarded. While this information-theoretic approach efficiently reduces token volume, it introduces a catastrophic vulnerability in long-document processing: the destruction of the semantic chain. Language encodes meaning through hierarchical, nested structures and long-distance dependencies. Function words, discourse markers, and pronouns frequently register low perplexity scores because their immediate syntactic roles are highly predictable. However, the semantic payload of an anaphoric or cataphoric pronoun (e.g., "he," "it," "the company") is entirely contingent upon its antecedent. When a middleware pipeline aggressively drops tokens, dense factual passages may be thinned out. If a primary entity is deemed statistically redundant in a localized chunk and is pruned, downstream pronouns are transformed into orphaned pointers. The generative LLM subsequently processing the compressed prompt attempts to resolve the pronoun, but because the referential target has vanished, the model hallucinates or fails the multi-hop reasoning task. This referential degradation severely impacts downstream accuracy, particularly on tasks requiring fine-detail extraction and episodic memory retention. To architect a compression pipeline capable of maintaining referential integrity while dropping up to 80% of tokens, the operational paradigm must shift. Texts can no longer be processed as linear sequences of statistically independent tokens; they must be modeled as directed semantic graphs where nodes (entities, events) and edges (coreferences, syntactic dependencies) are explicitly tracked, clustered, and preserved based on their network centrality and downstream utility.

## Ultra-Fast Coreference Normalization for Middleware Pipelines

Coreference resolution (CR)—the task of identifying text spans that refer to the same underlying entity—is a fundamental prerequisite for semantic graphing. Historically, integrating CR into real-time middleware has been computationally prohibitive. Traditional neural coreference systems treat the task as a complex clustering problem over span representations, requiring immense parameter counts and incurring profound latency. For instance, the coreference model implemented in the widely utilized AllenNLP library (based on Joshi et al., 2020) requires approximately 27GB of GPU memory and demands over 12 minutes to process the 2.8K documents of the OntoNotes corpus on a V100 GPU. Such overhead is entirely incompatible with millisecond-budgeted inference operations.

### The Fastcoref (F-Coref) and Ling Mess Architectures

The development of F-Coref and its underlying architecture, LingMess (Linguistically Informed Multi-Expert Scorers), marks a pivotal inflection point in the feasibility of inline coreference resolution. While large generative LLMs often struggle with precise mention detection—preventing their zero-shot coreference performance from surpassing supervised neural methods—supervised models trained on transformer backbones like DeBERTa have achieved state-of-the-art results. LingMess optimizes the supervised approach by summing individual mention scores across multi-expert models. However, to meet the strict latency requirements of a compression middleware, F-Coref utilizes model distillation to compress the strong-but-slow LingMess model using large unlabeled datasets. This distillation is paired with a highly optimized "leftover batching" implementation that drastically reduces the number of padded tokens within computational batches. The empirical results of this dual optimization are profound. F-Coref is capable of processing the entire 2.8K OntoNotes document corpus in merely 25 seconds. It operates 29 times faster than the AllenNLP equivalent and requires only 15% of the GPU memory footprint, while suffering only a marginal drop in average F1 score (78.5 versus the baseline 79.6).

## Implementation of Co-reference Normalization

In a robust prompt compression pipeline, an ultra-fast CR system like F-Coref enables "Co-reference Normalization" as a deterministic pre-processing stage. By identifying coreference clusters across the document at high speed, the middleware can execute direct string substitutions, replacing anaphoric expressions and pronouns with their canonical named entities. For example, cross-lingual implementations using tools like SpaCy's Coreferee demonstrate that complex multi-sentence dependencies—such as resolving the Polish pronouns "jego" (his) and "jej" (her) to their respective antecedents "Janek" and "pracą"—can be mapped explicitly into coreference chains. Once the text is fully normalized and all implicit pointers are replaced with explicit entities, aggressive perplexity-based token dropping algorithms can be applied safely. The destruction of any single entity mention no longer strands dependent pronouns further down the context window, because the pronouns have already been overwritten with the explicit entity name.

## Propositional Extraction and Structured Symbolic Rewriting

Moving beyond the probabilistic replacement of individual tokens, cutting-edge research has explored the structural reformulation of the text itself to ensure absolute referential stability. When a document is heavily compressed, the traditional boundaries of human-readable sentences collapse. To preserve semantic integrity, the underlying data structure can be converted into atomic, self-contained units optimized explicitly for machine ingestion.

## Dense X Retrieval and Propositional Units

The " Dense X Retrieval" methodology fundamentally shifts the unit of semantic processing and retrieval from passages or sentences to "propositions". A proposition is formally defined as an atomic expression within a text, encapsulating a distinct factoid and presented in a concise, self-contained natural language format. Crucially, the defining characteristic of a proposition is that it must include all necessary context from the surrounding text—specifically coreferences—to interpret its meaning independently. Sentences in long documents are frequently compounded and referentially complex; empirical analysis indicates that an average passage contains 6.3 distinct propositions, while an average sentence contains 2.3. To automate the segmentation of Wikipedia pages and enterprise documents into propositions, researchers fine-tune text generation models, commonly referred to as "Propositionizers". By prompting advanced models like GPT-4 to generate seed sets of propositions (e.g., yielding 42,857 training pairs), lightweight models such as Flan-T5-large can be fine-tuned to execute this extraction autonomously. This process actively reduces referential complexity. Comparative analyses reveal that reducing the number of pronoun chunks through propositional coreference resolution correlates directly with improved retrieval performance and long-context understanding in downstream QA tasks. Once a document is segmented into propositions, a middleware compressor can rank these atomic units by relevance and drop the lowest-scoring propositions entirely, ensuring that no orphaned semantic fragments remain in the active context.

## Telegraph English (TE): Bypassing Token-Level Ambiguity

A more radical and highly efficient evolution of propositional extraction is " Telegraph English" (TE), introduced in 2026 as a structured symbolic rewriting protocol for semantic prompt compression. TE completely bypasses the pitfalls of probabilistic token deletion by enforcing explicit grammatical structures that are machine-optimized. TE implements a strict, interlocked four-mechanism architecture, primarily characterized by a "one-claim-per-line" discipline and "upper-case entity naming". By consistently upper-casing the primary entities within a document and isolating individual factual claims to single lines, TE natively eliminates pronoun resolution ambiguity. Because the grammar is explicitly relational and symbolically structured, token deletion algorithms can operate on discrete claims rather than arbitrary tokens. This structural rigidity ensures that the deletion of a specific line does not strand a pronoun whose antecedent has been removed, as every line is referentially independent. Empirical evaluations on benchmark datasets (such as the Winograd Schema Challenge for pronoun resolution) demonstrate that at roughly a 50% token reduction, TE preserves an extraordinary 99.1% accuracy on key facts when processed by models like GPT-4.1. Across a suite of 4,081 QA pairs measuring key factual retention, an error analysis revealed that only 4.6% of items were correct on the original text but incorrect on the TE-compressed text for smaller models. Furthermore, TE consistently outperforms established statistical methods like LLMLingua-2 at matched compression ratios across every tested model and task. The performance gap is most pronounced on smaller capacity models—improving accuracy by up to 11 percentage points on fine-detail adversarial tasks—indicating that explicit relational structure actively compensates for limited model reasoning capacity. Additionally, TE employs adaptive compression based on information density. Rather than utilizing a fixed-ratio method that compresses dense and verbose passages identically, TE allows dense instructional passages to emerge at retention ratios near 1.0, while verbose exposition is crushed below a ratio of 0.2.

## Attention-Map Parsing for Mechanistic Dependency Preservation

While symbolic rewriting relies on external generative modules or fine-tuned Propositionizers, an alternative and profoundly efficient approach leverages the internal mechanics of the Transformer architecture itself. Attention-map parsing involves analyzing the internal attention weights computed during the LLM's forward pass to identify and preserve the implicit semantic graphs formed during processing.

## The Linguistic Topography of Attention Heads

Extensive research into the mechanistic interpretability of transformer models has revealed that specific attention heads naturally specialize in tracking linguistic syntax and coreference, effectively constructing an internal dependency tree. During self-attention, a head takes an input sequence of vectors corresponding to the tokens, transforming them into query ($q_i$), key ($k_j$), and value ($v_i$) vectors. The attention weights ($\alpha_{ij}$) between all pairs of words are computed as softmax-normalized dot products: $\alpha_{ij} = \exp(q_i^T k_j) / \sum \exp(q_i^T k_l)$. The resulting attention map is a matrix $M \in \mathbb{R}^{n \times n}$, where each entry $m_{i,j}$ represents the attention mass flowing from token $i$ to token $j$. Analysis of these matrices demonstrates that attention heads in the intermediate layers of models like BERT correspond remarkably well to linguistic properties. Specific heads consistently attend to the direct objects of verbs, the determiners of nouns, and the objects of prepositions. More importantly, specialized "coreference heads" can correctly identify coreferent mentions with up to 65% accuracy purely based on attention magnitude, a massive improvement over proximity-based heuristics (which average 27% accuracy). When aggregated, an attention-based probing classifier utilizing these maps achieves a 77 UAS (Unlabeled Attachment Score) at dependency parsing, proving that transformers build robust syntactic graphs natively. In a compression pipeline, this internal topography is invaluable. If a token possesses high incoming attention mass originating from multiple downstream tokens within these specialized coreference heads, it serves as a central node in the semantic graph. Consequently, it must be heavily weighted and protected from the pruning algorithm.

## TokenButler: High-Granularity Attention Distillation

Executing full forward passes of massive LLMs solely to calculate pruning metrics via attention-map parsing introduces severe latency, violating the constraints of real-time middleware. The TokenButler framework resolves this paradox by operating as an ultra-lightweight, high-granularity predictor trained to estimate token importance via attention distillation. TokenButler predicts low-dimensional importance queries ahead of time by distilling the masked causal attention distributions of a frozen LLM. During its training phase, the model runs a forward pass over a training corpus (e.g., C4-realnewslike) to extract the true pre-softmax attention logits ($A_{true}$), which serve as the ground truth. The predictor is then optimized using a Mean Squared Error (MSE) loss function ($L_{MSE} = ||A_{pred} - A_{true}||_2^2$) to minimize the disparity between predicted and actual attention scores. By employing a block-wise flash attention kernel to efficiently compute partial products without materializing large intermediate tensors, TokenButler maintains extreme memory efficiency. The resulting predictor incurs less than a 1.2% parameter overhead relative to the base LLM, yet provides query-aware estimates of which tokens will be critical for downstream coreference and decoding. Traditional KV-cache sparsity methods (such as StreamingLLM, H2O, or page-based eviction) often permanently evict tokens based on local attention metrics. This causes catastrophic page-misses if an evicted token later becomes necessary for resolving a co-reference. TokenButler, however, prioritizes tokens based on their predicted future persistence—calculating the mathematical probability that a token will continue to receive non-negligible attention as the decoding generation moves to different subproblems, entities, or topics. Evaluated on synthetic small-context (< 512 tokens) co-referential retrieval benchmarks designed to expose the failures of static eviction methods, TokenButler demonstrates near-oracle accuracy. Furthermore, by utilizing a prediction interval scheme that amortizes the predictor cost by 16x, TokenButler enables up to a 7.6x latency reduction over dense attention with CPU offloading, while improving perplexity and downstream accuracy by over 8% relative to state-of-the-art token importance estimators.

## Query-Dependent KV Cache Pruning (ThinK)

Building upon the foundations of attention-guided compression, advanced frameworks like ThinK (Thinner Key Cache by Query-Driven Pruning) introduce query-dependent pruning operating at the channel level rather than the token level. Instead of dropping an entire token and risking semantic rupture, ThinK exploits the fact that the channel dimension of the KV cache exhibits significant redundancy, characterized by an uneven, unbalanced magnitude distribution and a low-rank structure in the attention weights. By selectively pruning the least significant channels while minimizing overall attention weight loss, ThinK achieves a highly granular form of compression. When integrated with existing compression methods like KIVI, ThinK achieves a 2.8x reduction in peak memory usage without compromising output quality. This channel-wise sparsity allows inference servers utilizing single GPUs to increase their batch sizes by up to 5x, fundamentally altering the economics of long-context LLM deployment while preserving the query-dependent attention vectors necessary for coreference.

## Graph Convolutional Networks and Lightweight Dependency Trees

For middleware pipelines that prioritize text string manipulation over deep KV cache manipulation (i.e., "hard prompt" optimization methodologies), semantic stability must be maintained at the lexical level. If a heuristic compression algorithm evaluates and drops tokens individually, it is highly likely to break multi-word entities, idioms, noun phrases, and essential syntactic dependencies. SelectiveContext and Spa Cy Integration To safeguard structural coherence, prompt compression frameworks like SelectiveContext integrate lightweight dependency parsers. SelectiveContext operates by computing the self-information of lexical units via a base language model (such as GPT-2) to evaluate their informativeness. However, to maintain text coherence and prevent semantic fracturing, the algorithm utilizes the syntactic parsing capabilities of the SpaCy NLP pipeline prior to deletion. By leveraging SpaCy's dependency parser, SelectiveContext groups individual tokens into discrete noun phrases. Lexical units are clustered based on their dependency tree edges, forming indivisible blocks. This guarantees that if an entity is retained, it is retained in its entirety, preventing the fragmentation of complex subjects and ensuring that local syntactic dependencies—such as the linkage between an adjective and its head noun—are not severed. While highly effective for maintaining grammatical cohesion without relying on external large-scale models, reliance on traditional parsers introduces minor computational overhead and currently lacks robust methods for merging complex verb phrases.

## Edge-Enhanced Graph Convolutional Networks (EE-GCN)

For applications requiring more sophisticated semantic preservation, such as structural information extraction, researchers map documents into heterogeneous graphs. Traditional dependency tree parsers often ignore the nuances of edge labels (the specific semantic relationships between words). To resolve this, Edge-Enhanced Graph Convolutional Networks (EE-GCN) jointly model the syntactic dependency structures and the relation labels, capturing word interactions with substantially higher fidelity. When compressing prompts for complex reasoning, models like the DyGIE++ framework construct dynamic Span Graphs that update text representations across named entities, overcoming the error propagation issues inherent in pipeline methods and capturing long-distance dependencies effectively. In a topology-aware compression middleware, these frameworks trace the roots of a document's dependency tree. Nodes represent entities or tokens, and edges represent span-wise relationships. By applying node type matching and subgraph pattern matchers, the system can calculate degree centrality. If a node represents a central entity with a high frequency of downstream coreferential edges, it is assigned a maximal preservation priority, entirely immunizing the entity and its semantic chain against the token dropping algorithm.

## Dynamic Budget Allocation and Entity Clustering

A critical architectural flaw in early prompt compression systems was the reliance on fixed-ratio compression (e.g., uniformly dropping 50% of tokens across an entire document). Natural language is inherently heterogeneous; dense instructional text requires minimal compression to preserve directives, while redundant examples, verbose exposition, or highly repetitive semantic clusters can be aggressively pruned. Consequently, state-of-the-art pipelines utilize Dynamic Budget Allocation to distribute a specific "token preservation budget" optimally across different sections, entities, or transformer layers.

## LAVa: Layer-wise Cache Eviction with Dynamic Budgets

In the domain of KV cache compression, dynamic budgeting must occur inside the transformer layers during inference. The LAVa (Layer-wise KV Cache Eviction with Dynamic Budget Allocation) framework represents a significant breakthrough, offering a unified, training-free strategy for cache eviction. Rather than relying on heuristic thresholds or multiple combined metrics, LAVa minimizes information loss in the Transformer's residual streams by analyzing the Layer Attention Output Loss. LAVa calculates a precise scoring function that compares cache entries across different attention heads. By flattening the LAVa scores from all heads in a given layer into a one-dimensional array ($s_l$), the algorithm ranks cache entries to achieve a dynamic head budget. This allows the system to allocate significantly more memory to attention heads that are actively processing complex semantic relationships (such as the coreference heads identified in mechanistic interpretability studies) while heavily pruning redundant or uniformly-attending heads. Furthermore, by contrasting cross-layer information, LAVa dynamically adjusts the eviction budget per layer. Empirical evaluations across benchmarks like LongBench, Ruler, and InfiniteBench reveal a profound architectural insight regarding task-specific budgeting: dynamic layer budgets are paramount for generation tasks (such as code completion, where structural consistency over time is needed), whereas dynamic head budgets are critical for extraction tasks (where specific attention heads must be heavily funded to pull precise entities and their coreferences from the context). By operating as a fully dynamic method, LAVa consistently maintains top performance across varied task types.

## Entity-Aware Retrieval and Reallocation in Multi-Agent Systems

At the pre-computation level, dynamic budgets are used to protect highly referenced entities. Frameworks like the Deep Orchestrated Versatile Agent (DOVA) implement a multi-tiered, six-level token-budget allocation scheme based on entity-aware conversation contexts. By tracking the frequency of an entity and its downstream references within a semantic graph, DOVA adaptively redistributes sampling effort based on reward feedback. Similarly, for complex episodic reasoning, dynamic budget reallocation mechanisms transfer unused computational budget from sparse informational sections to complex targeted-extraction sections. Token preservation algorithms prioritize specific elements, guaranteeing that critical constraints and multi-pass entity retrievals receive the requisite token allowances. This paradigm is mirrored in multimodal models through frameworks like QuoTA and DBTrimKV, which evaluate visual and textual tokens based on query-oriented importance. They explicitly decompose redundancy, quantifying intra- and inter-document variation to allocate a content-aware token budget via Pareto selection procedures. Tokens belonging to dynamic foreground entities (or frequently referenced textual nouns) are clustered. The budget controller assigns these clusters "protected" status. Because the controller recognizes that utility fades quickly once generation moves to a different subproblem, entity, or topic, it enforces hard and soft limits—stopping when budgets are exceeded but reallocating resources when tracking core entities. If a specific entity holds a high degree of downstream syntactic dependencies, the token dropper is mathematically constrained, forced to allocate a larger portion of the compression budget to that cluster, preserving the integrity of the entity's network.

## Synthesizing an Ultra-Fast, Coreference-Aware Middleware Architecture

To effectively operationalize these distinct domains of research—high-speed coreference resolution, structural rewriting, attention distillation, dependency parsing, and dynamic budgeting—a modern middleware compression pipeline must be conceptualized as a multi-stage, topology-aware architecture. This architecture does not merely filter text based on linear probability; it actively compiles it into a dense, topologically resilient semantic format.

**Phase 1: High-Speed Coreference Normalization and Structuring** Upon the ingestion of a long document or multi-turn context, the middleware must instantaneously resolve referential ambiguity before any destructive operations occur. Utilizing a distilled neural model like F-Coref , all anaphoric pronouns and implicit references are identified and deterministically substituted with their canonical entities. For pipelines requiring extreme factual rigor (such as legal or medical RAG systems), a lightweight Propositionizer converts complex, convoluted sentences into self-contained propositions, explicitly eliminating context-dependency at the structural level. The document may then be formatted using the protocols of Telegraph English, isolating distinct claims and utilizing upper-case entity markers to signal referential nodes definitively to the downstream LLM.

**Phase 2: Syntactic Chunking and Dependency Graphing** To ensure the ensuing compression algorithm does not sever immediate linguistic structures, a rapid dependency parser (such as the SpaCy pipeline or a lightweight EE-GCN) maps the normalized text. The parser identifies noun chunks and establishes the foundational dependency tree. Lexical units are clustered based on these syntactic edges, forming indivisible blocks. This topological mapping guarantees that the subsequent pruning phase must either accept or reject complex entities as a whole, thereby preserving localized grammatical cohesion and preventing the corruption of multi-word named entities.

**Phase 3: Query-Aware Attention Prediction and Scoring** With the text structurally sound and referentially explicit, an attention distillation model like TokenButler scores the chunks. Because TokenButler operates with less than a 1.2% parameter overhead and utilizes block-wise flash attention kernels, it rapidly predicts the future persistence of tokens based on simulated true attention logits ($A_{true}$). Entities that serve as central nodes in the coreference graph receive exponentially higher persistence scores, as the predictor recognizes their mathematical necessity for resolving multi-hop downstream queries.

**Phase 4: Dynamic Budget Allocation and Eviction** Finally, the compression is executed. A dynamic budget controller evaluates the scores provided by the attention predictor alongside the density of the dependency trees. Using mechanisms akin to DOVA and LAVa, the controller allocates highly specific token and channel budgets across the document. Dense, fact-heavy segments containing tightly clustered, high-persistence entities are compressed minimally (e.g., a 10-20% reduction), while verbose, low-entropy segments with few dependency edges are compressed aggressively (up to an 80-90% reduction). If the pipeline operates at the KV-cache level during prefill, methodologies like ThinK are deployed to selectively prune low-rank attention channels, maximizing memory savings without discarding the tokens entirely.

## Conclusion

The pursuit of scaling Large Language Models to process massive context windows has catalyzed rapid innovation in prompt compression and KV cache management. However, the naive application of fixed-ratio or purely perplexity-driven token dropping critically undermines the semantic integrity of the prompt. When antecedent entities are arbitrarily excised, pronouns are stranded and dependency networks are severed, leading to hallucinatory degradation in RAG and reasoning tasks. As demonstrated by the convergence of recent advancements in natural language processing and mechanistic interpretability, the preservation of a document's semantic graph is fully achievable at middleware speeds. By integrating ultra-fast coreference resolution models (F-Coref), structural rewriting paradigms (Telegraph English, Propositional Units), and lightweight dependency parsers (SpaCy, EE-GCN), a compression pipeline can successfully immunize text against referential collapse. Concurrently, the deployment of attention-distilled predictors (TokenButler), query-dependent channel pruning (ThinK), and sophisticated, layer-wise dynamic budget controllers (LAVa, DOVA) ensures that computational resources are allocated precisely to the entities and semantic relationships that dictate downstream reasoning accuracy. Transitioning from sequence-level truncation to topology-aware, entity-preserving compression is no longer merely theoretical; it represents the foundational architectural shift required to make long-context LLM inference both economically viable and semantically robust at an enterprise scale.
